Module 6: Disk Management and Scheduling

Disk Management and Scheduling ensure efficient data access, optimized seek time, and fair I/O handling in operating systems.

Think of it as the OS being the traffic controller for disk requests üö¶‚Äî deciding who gets served first and how efficiently.

üß© Disk Components

  • Platter: Circular disk where data is stored magnetically.
  • Track: Concentric circles on the platter.
  • Sector: Subdivision of a track (smallest addressable unit).
  • Cylinder: Stack of tracks aligned vertically across platters.
  • Disk Arm (Actuator): Moves read/write head to the required track.
  • Head: Reads/writes data on the disk surface.

üìÄ Disk Access Time

  1. Rotational Latency ‚Üí Time for the desired sector to rotate under the head.
  1. Transfer Time ‚Üí Time to read/write actual data.

üß∞ 2. Disk Management Functions

üîπ Disk Initialization

  • Partitioning and formatting before use.
  • Formatting includes creation of file system structures (boot block, superblock, inode table).

üîπ Boot Block

  • Contains bootloader to start OS.
  • Located at a fixed location on disk.

üîπ Bad Block Management

  • Some sectors may become unreadable.
  • OS remaps or marks them as bad.
  • Modern disks use spare sectors for replacement automatically.

üí° Takeaway: Disk management ensures reliability and prepares disks for efficient storage.

üö¶ 3. Disk Scheduling Algorithms

Efficient scheduling = minimizing seek time and improving throughput.

üìö Real-life analogy:

A librarian fetching books (requests) from shelves (tracks). Smart route = less walking (seek time).

üî∏ 1. First-Come, First-Served (FCFS)

  • Serves requests in the order of arrival.
  • No reordering or optimization.

üìñ Example:

Requests: 40, 180, 10 ‚Üí Serve sequentially.

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Simple, fair.

‚ùå Disadvantages:

  • High total seek time.
  • Poor performance when requests are far apart.

üí¨ Takeaway: Easy but inefficient.

üîç Interview Tip: Often used as a baseline for comparison.

üî∏ 2. Shortest Seek Time First (SSTF)

  • Chooses nearest request from current head position.

üìñ Example:

If head at 50 ‚Üí Requests (10, 45, 80) ‚Üí pick 45 first.

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Lower total seek time.
  • Better throughput.

‚ùå Disadvantages:

  • Starvation possible for far requests.
  • Unfair in heavy load.

üí¨ Takeaway: Fast but not fair.

üîç Interview Tip: ‚ÄúHow does SSTF cause starvation?‚Äù ‚Äî explain head keeps choosing nearby requests.

üî∏ 3. SCAN (Elevator Algorithm)

  • Head moves in one direction, serving all requests.
  • At end ‚Üí reverses direction like an elevator.

üìà Movement Example:

If head moves 20 ‚Üí 80: serves requests in between; at 80, reverses.

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Uniform wait times.
  • Prevents starvation.

‚ùå Disadvantages:

  • Requests just missed at turnaround wait longer.

üí¨ Takeaway: Balanced algorithm for fairness and efficiency.

üîç Interview Tip: Compare SCAN vs SSTF ‚Äî trade-off between performance and fairness.

üî∏ 4. C-SCAN (Circular SCAN)

  • Moves in one direction only (e.g., outer ‚Üí inner).
  • At end ‚Üí jumps back to start without serving on return.

üìà Example:

20 ‚Üí 80 (serve requests), jump to 0, continue forward.

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Predictable wait times.
  • Uniform response distribution.

‚ùå Disadvantages:

  • Idle movement when jumping back.

üí¨ Takeaway: SCAN with circular efficiency.

üîç Interview Tip: "Why use C-SCAN over SCAN?" ‚Äî better for uniform service time under heavy load.

üî∏ 5. LOOK and C-LOOK

  • Optimized versions of SCAN and C-SCAN.

üìñ LOOK: Moves only up to last request in the direction ‚Üí reverses.

üìñ C-LOOK: Moves in one direction ‚Üí stops at last request ‚Üí jumps back.

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Avoids unnecessary traversal to empty ends.
  • Reduces total seek time.

‚ùå Disadvantages:

  • Slightly more complex implementation.

üí¨ Takeaway: Smarter SCAN and C-SCAN variants.

üîç Interview Tip: Explain the "LOOK" name ‚Äî the head looks ahead for farthest request.
